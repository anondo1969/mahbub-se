Mahbub Ul Alam
Data Engineer (AI) · Researcher · Builder
Stockholm, Sweden

I'm a Data Engineer (AI) at SciLifeLab Data Centre in Sweden, where I help life sciences and healthcare researchers leverage AI tools and platforms. My work spans building research infrastructure, developing AI agent workshops, and maintaining the SciLifeLab Serve platform, a hosting environment for 147+ public research applications.

Before this, I completed my PhD at Stockholm University (2018–2024), focusing on making AI in healthcare both practical and interpretable. My doctoral work connected clinical data with medical imaging to help doctors spot early signs of sepsis and COVID-19, earning two best paper awards along the way.

My journey started in Bangladesh, took me through Samsung R&D as a software engineer, then to the University of Stuttgart for a master's in Computational Linguistics, and eventually to Stockholm where I've been ever since. I find the convergence of natural language processing, healthcare AI, and AI research infrastructure especially compelling.

Looking ahead, I'm driven to utilize generative AI, large language models, AI agents, and agentic workflows, to create scalable, accessible, and impactful tools for the research and healthcare community.

Doctoral Thesis

Advancing Clinical Decision Support Using Machine Learning & the Internet of Medical Things: Enhancing COVID-19 & Early Sepsis Detection

Defended March 28, 2024 · Conferment Ceremony at Stockholm City Hall, September 27, 2024
📄 Read Thesis (PDF) 🔗 DiVA Portal 📊 Defense Slides

Abstract

This thesis examines the positive impact of Machine Learning (ML) and the Internet of Medical Things (IoMT) for advancing Clinical Decision Support Systems (CDSS) in COVID-19 and early sepsis detection. It emphasizes patient-centric healthcare systems requiring personalized and participatory care.

The integration of ML-driven CDSSs with IoMT is viewed as an extremely important step that could offer real-time decision-making support and enhance patient health outcomes. The thesis investigates ML's capability to analyze complex medical datasets, identify patterns and correlations, and adapt to changing conditions.

Key issues addressed cover data annotation scarcity, data sparsity, and data heterogeneity, along with aspects of security, privacy, and accessibility. The thesis also enhances the interpretability of ML prediction model-based CDSSs. Ethical considerations are prioritized to ensure adherence to the highest standards.

The thesis demonstrates the potential and efficacy of combining ML with IoMT to enhance CDSSs by emphasizing the importance of model interpretability, system compatibility, and the integration of multimodal medical data.

Keywords: Internet of Medical Things, Patient-Centric Healthcare, Clinical Decision Support System, Predictive Modeling in Healthcare, COVID-19, Sepsis, Early Sepsis Detection, Lung Segmentation, Interpretability, LIME, SHAP, Grad-CAM, LRP, Electronic Health Records, Machine Learning, Deep Learning, Federated Learning, Semi-Supervised Learning, Multi-Task Learning, Transfer Learning, Multi-Modality, NLP, ClinicalBERT.

SHAMSUL Demo App

Systematic Holistic Analysis to investigate Medical Significance Utilizing Local interpretability methods

SHAMSUL explores interpretability for chest X-ray pathology predictions using Grad-CAM, LIME, SHAP, and LRP. It provides heatmaps and evaluation metrics for better insights into the medical significance of deep learning predictions.

The acronym SHAMSUL, derived from a Semitic word meaning "the Sun," symbolizes our heatmap-based interpretability approach aimed at unveiling the medical significance in predictions of black box deep learning models.
